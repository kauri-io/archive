Wallet signature request messagesÔÉÅ

Caveat #1: I‚Äôll be talking about signature request messages in the context of logging in or accessing a dApp on the Ethereum blockchain, not when they‚Äôre used for verifying a smart contract transaction (that‚Äôll come later).

Wallet signature requests are some of the first things you come across when starting out with dApps. They are a crucial part of the decentralized ‚Äúlogging in‚Äù process. With some dApps, all you need is a wallet to access it. No account, no usernames, no passwords, no personal data stored by the dApp at all! A lot of the time you can enhance your experience by adding data afterwards, but it‚Äôs not a prerequisite for access.

Your wallet is your ticket to the blockchain party. It grants you access to a website‚Äôs blockchain features. So for example, if you head to The Bounties Network you can browse the bounties (tasks people are willing to pay for in crypto) right away. But to outsource or fulfil a bounty you need to be connected to the blockchain because they are fulfilled via smart contracts without middle-people. To connect to the blockchain you just need to log in using your wallet. That‚Äôs why we have signature request messages when logging in to a dApp. They‚Äôre essentially a security precaution and a bit like that bank verification modal you sometimes get when you pay for stuff online. Whether or not you agree with the metaphor, the importance of this verification step is without doubt. Yet the message is often complete gibberish, leading to unnecessary points of friction and confusion.

This is often the last step (hurdle?) in logging in, so it‚Äôs crucial. I think it‚Äôs time we started caring more about what it says.

The technical bitÔÉÅ

Under the hood a signature request is a dApp sending a small piece of data to the wallet and asking the user to go into the wallet and sign to say that they‚Äôve received it. This data might look like: d458fa15-dcab-4d85-a477-004d6febca12

Make sense?

After some sleuthing (and thanks to this tutorial ) I realised that this piece of data can be converted into something that can make sense and provide a better user experience. Hooray!

Personal_sign is a signature type ( see it in action ‚Äî you‚Äôll need MetaMask installed) that allows you to customise the message presented to your user. In the code it looks a little like this‚Ä¶

handleSignMessage = ({ publicAddress, nonce }) => {
    return new Promise((resolve, reject) =>
      web3.personal.sign(
        web3.fromUtf8(`I am signing my one-time nonce: ${nonce}`),
        publicAddress,
        (err, signature) => {
          if (err) return reject(err);
          return resolve({ publicAddress, signature });
        }
      )
    );
  };

I‚Äôve made it bold to show where you customise the message.

This will convert the random combination of letters and numbers into something a human can read. Now you‚Äôve just got to work out what to say.

The content bitÔÉÅ

I believe getting this message right can really improve the log in experience for both new and return users ‚Äî all in a really cheap way. Here‚Äôs my content design advice‚Ä¶

Don‚Äôt just pre-explain in your app You might be thinking, we have a screen for this in our dApp. But content belongs where it‚Äôs most contextually relevant. That‚Äôs why you should invest time in writing your MetaMask message ‚Äî that‚Äôs where your users are completing the sign action. If the MetaMask notification pops up automatically (as it sometimes does), your user may not even read your dApp screen. In fact, even if MetaMask doesn‚Äôt pop up automatically, they may not read it, especially if there‚Äôs no associated button or action to do. We all love skipping content when we think we can. However, definitely pre-explain the concept of message signing in your dApp too. With the space so new and the concept so different, over-explanation is probably a good idea. Make sure you direct your users to MetaMask when you ask them to sign the message ‚Äî it doesn‚Äôt always pop up.

Be human, have a zero tolerance policy on jargon Nonce doesn‚Äôt mean anything to regular users. And means something COMPLETELY different in the UK üò±. And why not say ‚ÄúHi‚Äù? Even though they‚Äôre interacting with MetaMask, this is still very much a space for your brand and your product.

Let your users know it‚Äôs free In the blockchain world, every on-chain event (a transaction that interacts with blockchain) requires a processing fee. The signature request is off-chain so doesn‚Äôt. Users might not understand this at first and be put off from connecting to your dApp. Be explicit that this won‚Äôt cost your users anything.

A good, but generic, message might look something like:
